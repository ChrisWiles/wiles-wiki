OpenAI and Anthropic have both moved toward a scenario-based view of AI economics, though they emphasize different parts of the problem.

## OpenAI

OpenAI's public documents emphasize broad benefit, democratized access, avoiding concentration of power, new economic models, infrastructure expansion, and shared upside.

Its [2023 AGI planning post](https://openai.com/index/planning-for-agi-and-beyond/) says AGI could increase abundance, turbocharge the global economy, and give people access to help with almost any cognitive task. It also argues that democratized access can decentralize power and that institutions need more capability and experience for complex AGI decisions.

Its [2026 principles](https://openai.com/index/our-principles/) are more explicitly political-economic. OpenAI says it will resist concentration of power, that key AI decisions should be made through democratic and egalitarian processes rather than only by labs, and that governments may need new economic models so everyone can participate in value creation. It ties universal prosperity to putting easy-to-use AI systems with substantial compute in many people's hands and driving infrastructure costs down.

OpenAI's [April 2026 industrial policy paper](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age/) is the closest thing to a concrete policy menu, but OpenAI labels it early and exploratory. The document says superintelligence may reshape work, knowledge, and production; warns about job and industry disruption plus concentrated power and wealth; and calls for democratic industrial policy rather than markets alone.

OpenAI-linked proposals and themes include:

- A right to AI or broad AI access for workers and entrepreneurs
- Public wealth funds or citizen stakes in AI-driven growth
- Modernized tax bases that shift away from labor and payroll taxes toward capital gains, corporate income, AI-driven returns, or automated labor taxes
- Faster cash support, wage insurance, training vouchers, and adaptive benefits
- Efficiency dividends, including shorter-week pilots without loss of pay
- Portable benefits detached from traditional employment
- Pathways into care, education, healthcare, and community services
- Energy and compute expansion with public benefit mechanisms

Sam Altman's earlier "American Equity Fund" proposal is a citizen-capital model: tax large companies and private land, then distribute money and shares to adult citizens. This is not just a welfare proposal; it directly addresses ownership. ^[inferred]

OpenAI's corporate structure is also relevant. Its nonprofit-control and public benefit corporation structure attempts to connect AI-generated enterprise value to public-benefit funding, though it is not the same as democratic public ownership. ^[inferred]

## Anthropic

Anthropic's public stance is less a single economic doctrine and more a combination of measurement, scenario planning, and warnings about labor-market disruption.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/economic-index) uses privacy-preserving Claude usage analysis to track how AI is used across occupational tasks. The March 2026 report found uneven adoption: Claude is used more intensely in high-income countries, in U.S. places with more knowledge workers, and in a relatively small set of specialized occupations. It also found that more experienced users attempt higher-value tasks and get more successful responses, which could make early adoption advantages self-reinforcing.

Anthropic's [Economic Futures Program](https://www.anthropic.com/news/introducing-the-anthropic-economic-futures-program) funds research and policy work on labor transitions, productivity, distribution, fiscal policy, social insurance, and measurement infrastructure. The program has three pillars: independent research, evidence-based policy development, and expanded economic measurement.

Anthropic's [October 2025 policy-response post](https://www.anthropic.com/research/economic-policy-responses) is scenario-dependent. Across scenarios, it emphasizes upskilling, worker-retention tax incentives, closing corporate tax loopholes, permitting reform, power infrastructure, economic measurement, and preparation. In moderate-disruption or fast-disruption scenarios, it raises AI adjustment assistance, automation taxes, compute or token taxes, sovereign wealth funds, VATs, and business wealth taxes.

Anthropic explicitly says these policy ideas do not all necessarily represent company positions. Its stronger stated commitment is to expose the range of plausible policy tools and support empirical work before the labor-market impacts are fully visible.

## Convergence

OpenAI and Anthropic converge on one point: if AGI shifts income from labor to capital, societies need institutions that distribute upside at the level of capital, compute, and public services, not only through worker retraining.

OpenAI leans toward broad access, universal prosperity, shared upside, and infrastructure. Anthropic leans toward measurement, uncertainty, labor-market monitoring, scenario planning, worker adjustment, and serious study of AI-related taxes and sovereign wealth mechanisms.
