A [[post-agi-economy|post-AGI economy]] is best treated as a scenario space. The decisive uncertainty is whether advanced AI remains mostly complementary to human work or becomes a broad substitute for cognitive, administrative, creative, scientific, and eventually physical labor.

The strongest research conclusion is not "UBI solves it" or "markets disappear." It is that labor income becomes an unreliable distribution mechanism if labor is no longer scarce. In that world, broad prosperity depends on ownership, access to compute, public services, tax capacity, competition policy, and institutions for purpose and status.

## Economic Structure

In current capitalism, most households obtain purchasing power by selling labor. Firms own productive assets, hire workers, pay wages, and sell output. Capital owners receive profits, interest, rents, and capital gains.

AGI could break this settlement if firms can produce much more output with much less human labor. Markets may still allocate scarce goods, but markets would no longer automatically distribute purchasing power broadly if most people have little labor income.

The formal economic anchor is Acemoglu and Restrepo's task model: automation displaces labor from tasks and tends to lower labor share, while new labor-using tasks can reinstate labor demand. The post-AGI worry is that AI may also perform many newly created tasks, weakening the usual historical reinstatement mechanism.

The relevant scarce inputs shift from ordinary labor toward [[ai-capital-ownership-and-purchasing-power|AI capital]]: compute, chips, energy, data centers, robotics, land, housing, health capacity, proprietary data, intellectual property, distribution, trust, regulation, and security.

## Plausible Models

**High-productivity labor economy.** AI automates tasks but not most whole jobs. Wages remain important, but inequality rises between workers who can use AI well and workers whose tasks are easy to automate. This is closest to current evidence from the IMF and Anthropic's Economic Index: high exposure in cognitive work, uneven adoption, and both augmentation and automation.

**Post-labor market capitalism.** Firms and markets remain, but AI systems perform most production. Wages shrink as a share of income; profits, rents, and capital gains dominate. The central question becomes who owns model weights, data centers, cloud infrastructure, robotics, energy, patents, platforms, and land.

**Shared AI infrastructure.** AI access becomes partly public utility, like electricity, internet access, libraries, public education, or roads. Private firms still innovate, but society guarantees a baseline of AI capability, compute access, training, and public-interest services.

**Citizen-capital economy.** Markets remain, but citizens own a share of AI-era capital through public wealth funds, sovereign wealth funds, AI dividends, capital grants, land-value revenues, or public stakes in AI infrastructure. This directly targets the distribution problem: if capital replaces labor, households need capital claims.

**AGI-centered capital economy.** In the most speculative model, AGI systems become major autonomous economic actors consuming compute, energy, robots, land, and materials. Korinek and Lockwood's public finance work treats this as a later stage in which labor income and even human consumption may become weak tax bases.

## Income And Purchasing Power

If employment is no longer the main way to distribute purchasing power, income must come from some mix of:

- Capital income and citizen dividends
- Public wealth or sovereign wealth funds
- Cash transfers, including UBI or negative income taxes
- Universal services
- Universal basic compute
- Public employment or civic contribution
- Grants, endowments, or universal basic capital

Falling production costs help, but only if the goods that matter become cheap enough. AI may make software, tutoring, entertainment, administrative work, and some professional services cheaper while housing, energy, land, healthcare capacity, insurance, and positional status remain scarce.

Consumer demand could weaken if wages fall faster than prices and redistribution. In a normal economy, wages are both a cost of production and the main source of demand. AGI lowers the cost side but can also remove the income channel. Whether demand collapses depends on price declines, capital-income concentration, credit conditions, and how quickly governments recycle AI rents back to households.

## Policy Toolkit

UBI is best understood as a floor, not a complete post-AGI settlement. The OpenResearch unconditional cash study found that cash increased flexibility and some forms of agency, but also produced modest reductions in employment and hours. That evidence supports cash as useful social insurance, not as proof that a full post-labor settlement can be cash-only.

The stronger policy portfolio includes:

- **Public wealth funds and citizen dividends:** broad claims on AI-driven capital returns.
- **Universal services:** healthcare, education, housing support, transport, child care, elder care, and AI tutoring to reduce cash dependence for essentials.
- **Universal basic compute:** direct access to AI capability as productive capacity, not just consumption.
- **Modern tax bases:** less reliance on labor/payroll taxes; more reliance on capital income, corporate income, rents, consumption, AI services, and possibly AGI capital.
- **Automation, token, digital services, robot, or compute taxes:** design should distinguish final consumption from productive capital. Korinek and Lockwood argue that token, robot-service, and digital-service taxes map better to early consumption taxation, while compute or robot capital taxes become more relevant if AI entities themselves absorb large resources.
- **Wage insurance, training, and adjustment assistance:** useful during transition, especially while human labor remains complementary.
- **Portable benefits:** healthcare, retirement, and training accounts detached from one employer.
- **Competition and interoperability:** prevent frontier models, cloud, chips, data, and distribution from consolidating into permanent oligopoly rents.
- **Public-interest infrastructure:** grid expansion, compute access, public research capacity, and public stakes in infrastructure.

## OpenAI's Public Position

OpenAI's February 2023 AGI planning post emphasized abundance, cognitive-task access, democratized deployment, public input, and stronger institutions for AGI decisions.

OpenAI's April 2026 "Our principles" is more explicit: it frames democratization as resisting concentration of power, says key AI decisions should use democratic and egalitarian processes, and argues that governments may need new economic models plus massive AI infrastructure to make prosperity widely shared.

OpenAI's April 2026 "Industrial policy for the Intelligence Age" is the most concrete OpenAI policy menu. It is explicitly early and exploratory. Its economic proposals include worker voice, AI-first entrepreneurship, a right to AI, tax-base modernization, a Public Wealth Fund, grid expansion, efficiency dividends such as four-day workweek pilots, adaptive safety nets, portable benefits, human-centered work, and AI-enabled scientific infrastructure.

OpenAI therefore frames the post-AGI transition as a democratic industrial-policy problem: keep markets and entrepreneurship, but add institutions that broaden access, share capital upside, update tax systems, and preserve social resilience.

## Anthropic's Public Position

Anthropic is more explicitly measurement-first and scenario-based. Its Economic Index tracks real Claude usage across tasks and occupations. Its March 2026 report found uneven adoption: more intense use in high-income countries, U.S. places with more knowledge workers, and specialized tasks. It also reported evidence that experienced users get better at using Claude, raising the risk of self-reinforcing adoption inequality.

Anthropic's Economic Futures Program funds empirical research, policy development, and economic data infrastructure. It is designed to support independent research on AI's labor, productivity, distributional, fiscal, and social insurance effects.

Anthropic's October 2025 policy-response post groups policies by scenario. Nearly all scenarios warrant upskilling, tax incentives for worker retention, closing corporate tax loopholes, and permitting reform for AI infrastructure. Moderate-disruption scenarios may require AI adjustment assistance and taxes on compute or token generation. Fast-moving scenarios raise sovereign wealth funds, VATs, and business wealth taxes.

Anthropic repeatedly notes that these ideas do not all represent definitive company policy. Its distinctive contribution is not a single preferred social model, but real-time measurement plus a policy menu matched to uncertainty.

## Social And Political Implications

Paid work currently provides income, schedule, identity, status, social ties, bargaining power, and contribution. Replacing wages without replacing these other functions would be politically fragile.

Optimistic post-AGI scenarios require new high-status non-market roles: caregiving, local governance, community service, arts, science support, education, environmental restoration, public-interest entrepreneurship, and democratic participation in AI governance.

The main political risks are wealth concentration, loss of labor bargaining power, weak demand, democratic erosion, geographic inequality, global inequality, and backlash from people who experience displacement as loss of status rather than only loss of income.

## Historical Context

Industrialization, electrification, computing, and globalization eventually raised living standards, but not automatically or evenly. Industrialization required unions, labor law, public education, antitrust, welfare states, and safety regulation to make growth politically legitimate. Computing and globalization lowered prices and created new sectors, but also weakened some communities and occupations.

The lesson is institutional, not deterministic: technology does not decide who benefits. Ownership, tax systems, labor institutions, competition policy, public services, and political power do.

AGI may differ because it targets general cognitive labor and potentially the process of creating new tasks. Historical analogies are useful, but weaker if AI can automate the very work that previously absorbed displaced labor.

## Bottom Line

A plausible post-AGI economy can still use markets, prices, entrepreneurship, and private firms. But if human labor is no longer the main scarce input, wages cannot remain the main mechanism for broad purchasing power.

The most defensible preparation strategy is a portfolio: broad AI access, public-interest compute, shared capital ownership, public wealth funds, universal services, adaptive cash support, tax reform for AI rents and capital income, competition policy, worker-transition systems, and respected institutions for non-market contribution.
