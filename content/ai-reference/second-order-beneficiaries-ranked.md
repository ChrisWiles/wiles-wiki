This is investment research, not financial advice. The framing complements [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]] and [[ai-investment-public-company-scorecard]] by stepping one rung removed from the obvious electrical-equipment, optics, and memory winners.

## Framing

The first-order AI buildout names — Eaton, Schneider, Hitachi, GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Vertiv, Micron, Broadcom, Coherent, Lumentum, Quanta, EMCOR — are increasingly priced as AI infrastructure. The second-order beneficiaries are upstream of those: copper out of the ground, electrical and structural steel, the gas that fires the turbines, the pipes that deliver the gas, the batteries that ride through outages, the fuel cells that bypass the utility queue, the diesel and gas reciprocating engines, the aggregates poured under every slab, the water cooling the chillers, and the scarce craft labor that ties it together.

These names often carry weaker AI narrative discounts because their revenue is diversified across construction, transportation, manufacturing, or commodity cycles. That dilution is also the trap: a stock can be a "real" AI beneficiary on the demand margin while being an unreliable AI proxy by mix. Investability therefore depends as much on purity as on scarcity.

The right way to read this page is as a complement to [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]] (which ranks first-order names) and [[ai-investment-public-company-scorecard]] (which scores both). The supply-chain map in [[ai-data-center-supply-chain-bottlenecks]] anchors the bucket structure used below. Where appropriate, the analysis below cross-references the regional power tightness story in [[us-power-region-deep-dive]] and the equipment-side scarcity story in [[electrical-equipment-deep-dive]]. Tripwires that would invalidate this thesis are tracked in [[risk-tripwires-and-warning-indicators]].

## Scoring framework

Three axes, each scored 1-5. Composite tier is set by the sum, then adjusted for liquidity and conviction.

**Scarcity (S, 1-5).** How tight is the underlying capacity? 5 = single-digit-percent capacity headroom, multi-year lead times, or a structural deficit that does not flex with price. 1 = ample, fast-flexing capacity.

**Purity (P, 1-5).** What share of revenue or contribution margin is actually levered to the AI buildout? 5 = >50% of revenue tied to AI/data center customers or to a clearly AI-driven commodity tightness. 1 = <10% of revenue with no clear path to higher mix.

**Valuation vs 5-year history (V, 1-5).** 5 = trading near the cheap end of its 5-yr EV/EBITDA or P/E range. 1 = at or above 5-yr highs with little room to rerate. ^[inferred]

Composite tiers: Tier 1 = 11-15 with at least one 4+ on each of S and P. Tier 2 = 8-10 or any axis at 1. Tier 3 = below 8 or any axis at 1 plus weak narrative.

## Copper

The single best second-order story by physics: an AI hyperscale campus can use up to roughly 50,000 tonnes of copper per site, and AI data centers are forecast to consume on the order of 500,000 tonnes per year by 2030 against an LME copper price that already hit an all-time high near $13,300/t in early January 2026. ^[extracted] Q1 2026 LME prices ranged from roughly $11,925 to $13,500/t. ^[extracted]

**FCX (Freeport-McMoRan).** Largest US-listed copper pure play, but the purity story is "global copper", not "AI". 2026 sales guidance was cut to roughly 3.1 billion pounds from 3.4 billion as Grasberg ramp slipped; Q1 2026 realized copper price was $5.78/lb. ^[extracted] Morgan Stanley moved to Equal-Weight at a $66 PT. ^[extracted] Scarcity 4, Purity 3, Valuation 3.

**SCCO (Southern Copper).** Among the lowest cash-cost majors and a reliable dividend payer, but Buenavista and Tia Maria carry permitting and political-risk overhangs in Mexico and Peru. The AI bid lifts the entire copper curve, so SCCO benefits without taking project-specific risk; the trade-off is that SCCO already trades closer to the rich end of its 5-yr range. ^[inferred]

**ERO (Ero Copper).** Brazilian small-cap with the Tucumã ramp as the dominant swing factor. High operating leverage to spot copper makes ERO the highest-beta listed name in the bucket. Purity is high relative to majors — copper dominates the revenue mix — but liquidity and country risk constrain position sizing. ^[inferred]

**TECK (Teck Resources).** Now a copper pure play after divesting steelmaking coal. QB2 throughput and the Highland Valley life-extension are the operational swing factors. The post-divestiture rerating is partly done; further upside depends on QB2 reaching nameplate. ^[inferred]

The copper bucket is structurally Tier 2 because no single listed name combines high purity with low country risk and below-history valuation. The right way to express the AI copper thesis may be a basket rather than a concentrated single-name bet. ^[inferred]

## Electrical steel and iron

The transformer story is the AI story for electrical steel. Cleveland-Cliffs is the only US producer of grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES) at Butler, PA, and is repurposing its Half Moon facility in Weirton, WV into a $150M three-phase distribution transformer plant — using under-utilized capacity to lift GOES tonnage by 30-40%. ^[extracted] That makes CLF a true scarcity node and arguably the cleanest second-order purity story in the table.

**NUE / STLD / X.** Hyperscale builds consume upwards of 20,000 tons of structural steel per campus, which has helped drive a record backlog for Nucor's steel mill products and a 35% jump in Steel Dynamics' order backlogs into Q1 2026. ^[extracted] Both are still primarily exposed to broader nonresidential and infrastructure demand, so purity is moderate. US Steel sits in post-Nippon limbo; treat X as ambiguous on disclosure quality. ^[ambiguous]

## Gas E&P, midstream, and pipelines

This is where the buildout cycle most clearly bends a commodity curve. Williams (WMB) has $5.1B of committed Power Innovation capex, with the $2B Socrates project (440 MW, Meta PPA in Ohio) online H2 2026 and Apollo + Aquila representing a combined ~$3.1B for H1 2027 — each backed by 10-year take-or-pay contracts. Williams cited 6 GW of additional projects under evaluation. ^[extracted]

EQT signed an agreement in principle to supply up to 665,000 MMBtu/d of gas to the 4.4 GW Homer City AI campus, alongside ~800 MMcf/d to Shippingport, plus 1.2 Bcf/d previously announced to Duke and Southern — totaling roughly 2.6 Bcf/d of potential commitments. ^[extracted] EQT screens as the highest-purity Appalachia E&P play on AI gas demand.

**KMI (Kinder Morgan).** Largest US gas pipeline footprint by miles. The data center thesis runs through expansion projects on existing rights of way — the durable advantage is permitted corridor scarcity, not the headline ticker. Purity is diluted by refined products and CO2 segments. ^[inferred]

**OKE (ONEOK).** NGL-heavy mix dilutes the AI gas-to-power story, but Permian and Mid-Con throughput compounds with industrial and power-load growth. Useful as a diversified gas/NGL midstream anchor rather than a pure AI proxy. ^[inferred]

**ET (Energy Transfer).** Texas intrastate volumes and signed data center MOUs make ET the most direct ERCOT data center play among large MLPs, but governance and payout structure depress multiple. ^[inferred]

**TRGP (Targa).** Permian gas takeaway and NGL fractionation. Indirect AI exposure: more associated gas and NGLs flow as Permian power demand grows, but the AI label is a stretch.

**AR (Antero Resources).** Higher-beta Appalachian gas analog to EQT. Less directly contracted to data centers so far, but a clean way to express rising Marcellus/Utica realized prices if EQT-style deal flow expands. ^[inferred]

## Pipelines, gas LDCs, and gas-to-power adjacencies

ETRN merged into EQT in prior cycles; the Mountain Valley Pipeline expansion is the residual asset story. The cleanest expression of MVP-style basis tightening now runs through EQT directly. ^[inferred]

Among LDCs, Atmos (ATO) and NJR (NJR) get a small data center kicker but are dominated by retail rate-base growth — purity is low. ^[inferred] ATO has Texas exposure to a region adding meaningful new load; NJR has Mid-Atlantic exposure to PJM data center alley but a small system relative to PJM's largest LDCs. These are Tier 3 unless data center load mix becomes large enough to move overall ROE growth materially. The cleaner regulated-utility plays on AI load are vertically integrated power utilities like Constellation, Vistra, AEP, Duke, and Southern — those sit in [[us-power-region-deep-dive]] and are first-order, not second-order.

## Water and aggregates

Data centers used about 17.4 billion gallons of water in the US in 2023, projected to reach 38-73 billion gallons by 2028. ^[extracted] AWWA's "Cooling the Cloud" white paper details the operational and infrastructure stress on water utilities planning for these loads. ^[extracted] But most water demand flows through fragmented municipal systems, not a single listed utility, which structurally caps purity for any public water name.

**AWK (American Water).** Most diversified regulated water utility footprint with the largest planned capex program in the sector. Useful as a hedged way to express data center water-stress without taking single-region geological risk, but AI is at most a 5-15% kicker on rate-base growth. ^[inferred]

**AWR (American States Water).** Smaller, California-concentrated, and less differentiated. Tier 3.

Aggregates are a cleaner story. Martin Marietta (MLM) reported data center demand up roughly 60% in Q4 2025 and guided 2026 revenue to $6.42-6.78B with EPS up ~19% y/y. ^[extracted] The thesis is that data center pads, transmission corridors, substation foundations, and access roads compound on top of an already strong public-works tailwind. Vulcan Materials (VMC) carries similar Sunbelt exposure with a 2026 EPS estimate up roughly 14.6%. ^[extracted] Both rerated alongside the broader infrastructure trade; valuation is the binding constraint and is the reason MLM lands at Tier 1 only on the strength of explicit data center disclosure.

## Batteries and energy storage

Fluence Energy (FLNC) printed a record ~$5.5B backlog at end-2025, a 2026 revenue outlook of $3.2-3.6B (~85% covered), and a pipeline that grew ~30% to $30B with over 30 GWh in data center projects, the majority behind-the-meter and over 80% originated in Q4 2025. ^[extracted] That mix makes FLNC the cleanest listed second-order play on AI battery storage. The risk is FEOC compliance under evolving US content rules and the rate at which behind-the-meter data center storage transitions from pilot to standard.

Eos Energy (EOSE) is a higher-risk zinc-bromine alternative — long duration positioning is real but backlog conversion and gross margin path remain unproven at scale. ^[inferred] Tier 3 until the unit economics print.

Tesla Energy is a real Megapack beneficiary — Megapack deployments and Lathrop/Shanghai capacity expansion are the visible variables — but it is buried inside an auto-led equity story, so purity as an "AI battery" is structurally low. ^[inferred] Treat as a proxy, not a clean expression.

## Fuel cells and onsite power

Bloom Energy (BE) is the standout. The 2.5 GW Oracle Project Jupiter agreement, the AEP $2.65B / 1 GW take-or-pay, and roughly $7.65B in contracts secured in a single 90-day period in early 2026 are concrete enough to move the multi-year demand curve. ^[extracted] Bloom is targeting 2 GW of annual production capacity. ^[extracted] Scarcity 5, Purity 5; valuation has rerated meaningfully and is now the binding constraint. The relevant risk is execution — can Bloom physically build and commission stack capacity at the contracted pace — and that is now the metric to watch quarterly.

**PLUG (Plug Power).** Hydrogen-focused; data center applications remain experimental. Cash burn is the dominant risk and the AI angle is more narrative than backlog. ^[inferred] Tier 3.

**BLDP (Ballard Power).** PEM stack supplier with stationary data center pilots but no large signed AI offtake. ^[inferred] Tier 3.

**BWXT.** A separate bet on small modular reactor (SMR) forging and naval nuclear backlog. The AI angle is real — SMRs are one of the few credible behind-the-meter answers above 100 MW — but multi-year and lumpy. The valuation has already moved with the SMR narrative, so the rerating is largely done. ^[inferred]

## Engines and generators

Caterpillar's $51.2B backlog and ~125% expansion of data center engine manufacturing capacity over the past two years frame the fulfillment problem. ^[extracted] The "Monarch" 2 GW deal cemented data centers as a structural Energy & Transportation segment driver. ^[extracted] The bull case is that gas reciprocating engines >330 kVA are the fastest-growing power gen segment, and units >750 kVA are forecast to take roughly a quarter of the total market by 2034. ^[extracted] CAT's diversified mining and construction exposure dilutes purity but supports the multiple in cyclical drawdowns.

Cummins reported a Power Systems data center revenue path toward roughly $3.5B in 2026 and recent margin records (EBITDA margins ~22.7%). ^[extracted] CMI is structurally cheaper than CAT on data-center-only mix and offers a cleaner second-order purity story.

Generac (GNRC) launched a 2.25-3.25 MW industrial generator lineup (diesel and gas) targeting hyperscale, colocation, and edge, and announced an Enercon Engineering acquisition in February 2026 to add switchgear and enclosure capacity. ^[extracted] The thesis: Generac is the public small-cap pure play on the same secular shift that benefits CAT and CMI, with a cleaner Industrial mix story but more execution risk on capacity adds. ^[inferred]

Kohler (private) and Mitsubishi Heavy MGS are the other large reciprocating-engine players but only CAT, CMI, and GNRC are cleanly investable. There is no Korean reciprocating-engine pure play that screens cleanly here; the ticker reference "KOEP" was non-canonical. ^[ambiguous] For exposure to data center generator demand outside the US majors, Mitsubishi Heavy is captured under the first-order power generation list in [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]] rather than this second-order page.

## Construction and EPC

Quanta (PWR) reported Q1 2026 revenue of $7.87B and a record $48.5B backlog, raised 2026 revenue guide to $34.95B at the midpoint and EPS guide to $13.90, and explicitly flagged craft labor tightness — particularly for data centers — as a binding execution risk. ^[extracted] Comfort Systems (FIX) carries a $12.5B backlog (~$5.3B y/y same-store growth, exceeding FY25 revenue) and guided mid-to-high 20% same-store growth for 2026. ^[extracted] EMCOR's RPOs hit $15.62B and management called Network & Communications activity "unprecedented". ^[extracted]

These are partly first-order names — the market clearly understands data center exposure. The second-order angle is the labor scarcity itself: the binding constraint moves from gear and gas to electricians, mechanical fitters, commissioning agents, and substation crews. MTZ, STRL, ACM, J, and FLR widen the bench but with more diversified mixes.

**MTZ (MasTec).** Clean Energy and Power Delivery segments are the AI-relevant exposures; Communications drag is a known overhang. Purity has improved as Clean Energy backlog has built. ^[inferred]

**STRL (Sterling Infrastructure).** E-Infrastructure Solutions includes data center site work as a major mix component. Among small-caps, Sterling has the cleanest data center print, but the stock has rerated meaningfully so valuation is the binding constraint. ^[inferred]

**ACM (AECOM), J (Jacobs), FLR (Fluor).** Design and program management plays. Purity is lower because the revenue is project services, not delivery capacity, and the customer mix spans federal, transportation, and water. Better as risk-managed adjacencies than as core AI bets. ^[inferred]

## Land and power-adjacent real estate

Kilroy Realty (KRC) was cited in *Situational Awareness LP* disclosures and screens as office/life-science by mix, not data center; the Q1 2026 earnings frame the business around the 1900 Broadway Redwood City development and an FFO range of $3.25-3.45, with no specific data center mix disclosed. ^[extracted] Treat the AI angle as optionality, not thesis. ^[ambiguous]

WPC is a diversified net-lease REIT with limited direct AI mix; better as a yield vehicle than as an AI proxy. ^[inferred]

The cleaner public expression is Brookfield Infrastructure (BIP): 1.6 GW of contracted data center capacity, 3.5 GW of development potential, an inaugural 55 MW behind-the-meter project under a Bloom framework agreement now expanded to ~230 MW across multiple hyperscalers on 15-year terms. ^[extracted] BIP guided to 10%+ FFO per unit growth in 2026 and benefits from parent BAM's $100B AI infrastructure program backed by Nvidia and KIA among others. ^[extracted] BIP screens as Tier 2 today because the data center segment is still a minority of total FFO; it could migrate to Tier 1 if the contracted GW continues compounding at the current pace.

The pure-play listed data center REITs (Equinix, Digital Realty) are first-order and live in the [[ai-investment-public-company-scorecard]]. The "land scarcity" angle worth tracking separately is power-secured brownfield and converted crypto sites, which sit in the highest-beta basket discussed in [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]].

## Tier 2 / Tier 3 watchlist

- Tier 2: FCX, SCCO, ERO, TECK, NUE, STLD, KMI, OKE, ET, TRGP, AR, AWK, VMC, FLNC, BWXT, GNRC, MTZ, STRL, ACM, BIP.
- Tier 3: X, ATO, NJR, AWR, EOSE, PLUG, BLDP, J, FLR, KRC, WPC, TSLA-as-storage-proxy.

Tier 2 names are watchlist candidates where the thesis is plausible but either purity is moderate, valuation has already absorbed near-term news, or the data center mix is not yet a needle-mover for total earnings. Tier 3 names are tracked but should be entered only on a specific catalyst — a signed multi-GW data center contract, a step-change in disclosed AI revenue mix, or a meaningful valuation reset.

The migration logic between tiers: a Tier 2 name moves to Tier 1 when (a) a specific data center contract or commodity tightness is disclosed in management commentary, (b) the share of revenue or contribution margin tied to AI buildout crosses ~30%, or (c) the valuation backs off to the cheaper end of its 5-yr history without any deterioration in the underlying scarcity story. Most Tier 1 names today became Tier 1 in the past 9-12 months on exactly that pattern.

## What looks already priced in

- **PWR, FIX, EME** — backlog, mix, and AI narrative are well understood; rerating is mostly behind us. Future returns track execution and labor inflation, not multiple expansion. The Q1 2026 prints (Quanta $48.5B backlog, Comfort $12.5B backlog, EMCOR $15.6B RPOs) all reinforced this, but multiples already reflect them. ^[extracted]
- **VMC and MLM** — already rated as Sunbelt infrastructure plays; further upside requires continued data center mix expansion above current run-rate. MLM's 60% Q4 data center demand growth datapoint helped re-anchor expectations rather than reset them. ^[extracted]
- **BE** — multiple has already absorbed the Oracle, AEP, and Brookfield wins; further upside requires production capacity execution and additional multi-GW signings, not narrative discovery. The 2 GW production capacity target is the binding gating variable. ^[extracted]
- **CAT and CMI** — diesel/gas reciprocating engine demand is mainstream consensus; the cycle risk is post-buildout normalization, not whether AI is real. Watch for any deceleration in CAT's E&T order book as the leading indicator.
- **STRL** — small-cap E-Infrastructure mix has already been rerated. Upside requires multi-year backlog visibility extension, not just the current data center mix.
- **BWXT** — SMR forging narrative has driven much of the move. The thesis is durable but the easy multiple expansion is behind.

The cleaner remaining mispricings live where the sub-bucket has not yet been collectively re-rated as "AI": CLF (GOES purity), EQT and WMB (specific contract economics still being underwritten as old-energy multiples), FLNC (battery storage often classified as renewables not AI infra), and BIP (still a yield vehicle in many models).

Three meta-observations:

1. The cleanest second-order purity stories are commodities with a single binding chokepoint and one or two listed pure plays — GOES (CLF), Appalachia gas with signed data center supply (EQT), gas-to-power infrastructure with take-or-pay contracts (WMB), and SOFC fuel cells (BE). Everything else is a basket trade.
2. The labor scarcity story (PWR/FIX/EME) is real but already understood. The asymmetry now is in the next layer down — apprenticeship pipelines, prefab modular construction, and commissioning capacity — which is mostly private.
3. The biggest valuation traps in this list are names where AI is a real demand kicker but a small share of total revenue (NUE, STLD, OKE, ET, AWK, ACM, J, FLR, KRC, WPC). Owning these as "AI plays" is mix-trapping; owning them for the underlying business and treating AI as optionality is more defensible.

## Final view

The second-order beneficiaries are not a substitute for the first-order picks — they are a complement. The disciplined construction is to combine a high-purity first-order basket (electrical equipment, gas turbines, optics, memory) with a smaller, deliberately selected second-order basket targeting (a) GOES and copper, (b) Appalachia gas with signed data center supply, (c) gas-to-power midstream under take-or-pay, (d) SOFC fuel cells, (e) Sunbelt aggregates, and (f) labor-constrained EPC.

The thesis fails if model efficiency, hyperscaler capex discipline, or interconnection reform compresses the buildout into a normal cloud cycle. The thesis succeeds in proportion to how durable and physically constrained each chokepoint actually proves to be. Most second-order names rerate slower than first-order names but also normalize slower if the thesis holds, which is the structural reason they are worth carrying alongside the obvious picks.
