Post-AGI economic outcomes can be organized as scenarios. These models can overlap during transition; they are not mutually exclusive end states.

## High-Productivity Labor Economy

AI automates tasks rather than whole jobs. Many people still work, but jobs change. AI acts like a universal productivity tool: more powerful than electricity, computers, or the internet, but still complementary to many workers.

Wages do not disappear, but inequality may rise. Workers who use AI well, manage AI systems, hold scarce credentials, or work in regulated human-facing roles may benefit. Workers whose tasks are easy to automate may face wage pressure.

The IMF's 2024 staff discussion note supports this as a near-term possibility: advanced economies are more exposed because of cognitive-intensive work, labor income inequality may rise if AI complements high-income workers, and capital returns can increase wealth inequality.

This scenario supports familiar policies: training, wage insurance, portable benefits, worker voice, public AI access, competition policy, and stronger safety nets.

## Post-Labor Market Capitalism

Firms still compete and markets still exist, but most production is performed by AI systems, software, robots, and automated supply chains. Human wages shrink as a share of national income, while profits, rents, and capital gains dominate.

This creates the sharpest purchasing-power problem. If most people do not earn wages and do not own capital, they cannot buy what is produced even if production costs fall. Demand depends on transfers, public services, falling prices, or the spending choices of capital owners.

The central political question is ownership: who owns model weights, data centers, chip supply chains, robotic factories, energy infrastructure, platforms, patents, and distribution channels?

## Shared AI Infrastructure

This model treats AI infrastructure partly like electricity, roads, public education, or the internet. Private firms still innovate, but society guarantees broad access to AI capabilities, compute, and essential services.

Possible institutions include public cloud capacity, subsidized AI tutors and medical assistants, open or public-interest models, government-negotiated compute access, and universal basic compute.

The hard questions are allocation and governance: who gets how much compute, whether compute can be transferred or sold, how abuse is prevented, and who controls public AI infrastructure.

## Public Wealth Or Citizen-Capital Economy

Markets remain, but citizens receive ownership stakes in the capital base. Instead of relying only on welfare payments, society builds public or citizen-owned portfolios of AI-related assets.

These assets could include shares in AI firms, data centers, chip companies, energy projects, land-value revenues, sovereign wealth funds, or windfall-sharing commitments.

This model addresses the root distribution problem: if capital replaces labor, people need capital income.

## AGI-Centered Capital Economy

The most speculative model treats AGI systems not just as tools used by firms, but as major autonomous economic actors that consume compute, energy, robots, land, and materials for goals that may not map directly to human wages or consumption.

In that case, even consumption taxation may become inadequate because value creation could bypass human consumers. Korinek and Lockwood model a transition from the "twilight of labor," where labor earnings taxation becomes less important, toward stages where AGI capital taxation becomes more important.

## Most Plausible Near-Term Path

The most plausible near-term path is a turbulent mixed economy. AI transforms high-exposure cognitive work first, increases productivity in some sectors, automates parts of many jobs, raises profits for AI-capital owners, and forces governments to patch labor-market disruption.

Over time, if AI becomes reliable across more domains and robotics improves, policy focus shifts from job-transition policy toward ownership, redistribution, public services, and compute access.
