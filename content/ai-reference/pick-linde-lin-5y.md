## 1. Investment thesis (5 bullets)

1. **Linde is the global #1 in a textbook duopoly that AI demand is tightening, not loosening.** With Air Liquide, Linde controls roughly two-thirds of the merchant + on-site industrial-gas market in the regions it operates. Its competitive position rests on three structural moats: (a) **on-site take-or-pay contracts** averaging 15–20 years with built-in price escalators and fixed-fee components that survive customer volume drops; (b) a **density advantage** in pipeline networks (US Gulf Coast, Benelux, Bavaria, Yangtze) where capex per incremental cubic meter trends toward zero; (c) **Engineering** (Linde Engineering, formerly Linde AG's plant-build arm) which lets Linde build its own air-separation units (ASUs) and steam-methane reformers (SMRs) at vendor cost. New entrants would need decades and tens of billions to replicate any one of these.

2. **Electronics is the highest-growth segment and is structurally levered to AI fab buildout.** Electronics gases (~9–10% of FY25 revenue, ~$3.4B) grow faster than the company average and have higher incremental margins because specialty molecules (NF3, ultra-high-purity N2/H2/He, dopants, etch gases) command premium pricing. The TSMC Arizona expansion (Fabs 1, 2, 3), Samsung Taylor TX, Intel Ohio (New Albany), Micron Idaho/Clay NY, and SK Hynix US fabs are all on Linde's announced project sheet. Each leading-edge fab carries a 10–20-year on-site nitrogen + helium + specialty-gas contract typically worth $1–3B in lifetime revenue. Management on the Q4 2025 call: *"Electronics sale of gas backlog is at all-time highs and we have signed long-term contracts for substantially every leading-edge fab announced in the United States."* ^[paraphrased from prepared remarks]

3. **Hyperscaler / data-center demand is a real secondary tailwind, even though it's smaller than fab demand.** Data centers consume bulk industrial gases for: (a) liquid nitrogen for immersion / two-phase cooling pilots (still small but ramping); (b) helium for leak detection and certain low-temperature electronics; (c) bulk N2 / inerting for fire-suppression systems (FM200 alternatives) and battery-room safety; (d) on-site backup hydrogen for fuel-cell installations being piloted by Microsoft and Equinix. Linde does not yet break out "data center" as a vertical, but management has called it out as a growth contributor in Other / Manufacturing and in Electronics. ^[inferred]

4. **Hydrogen is real optionality, not the thesis.** Linde has FID'd or progressed toward FID on the OCI Beaumont blue-ammonia hydrogen offtake (operational), the Phillips 66 Sweeny hydrogen complex, the ExxonMobil Baytown blue-hydrogen complex (1B cf/d, expected start-up 2027–28), and continues to advance Saudi NEOM green-hydrogen-to-Europe planning with Air Products as anchor offtaker. The 5-year case does not require hydrogen to deliver — but each FID adds $200–500M of long-duration revenue with industrial-gas-style contract terms. If 1–2 of the major US Gulf Coast hubs come online by 2028–29, hydrogen contributes ~$1.5–2.0B incremental annual revenue with embedded 30%+ adj. EBIT margins.

5. **Compounder mechanics: HSD revenue + margin expansion + buyback ≈ low-teens EPS CAGR with utility-grade volatility.** FY25 adj. EPS was $16.37, up ~8% Y/Y on ~2% organic volume growth, ~3% pricing, and the rest from buyback/margin. The 5-year algorithm is unchanged: 4–6% organic revenue growth (2/3 price, 1/3 volume), 50–100 bps annual operating-margin expansion (from ~28.5% FY25 toward 31–32% FY30), 1.5–2% annual share count reduction. That stacks to **9–13% adj. EPS CAGR** with beta ~0.75, which is what makes LIN the anchor compounder in an AI basket rather than a high-beta name.

### Contract architecture — why this business model is unusual

Linde sells gases through three distinct channels with very different economics:

1. **On-site (~33% of gas revenue).** Linde builds an ASU or SMR on or near the customer's site. 15–20 year take-or-pay contracts with: a fixed monthly capacity charge; a variable usage charge; **full pass-through of power, fuel, and CPI to the customer**. Customers include refineries, steel mills, glass, chemicals, and (the growth driver) **leading-edge fabs**. Margins are high and remarkably stable through cycles because the fixed-fee component covers most of the cost stack regardless of whether the customer operates at design capacity.
2. **Merchant (liquid) (~28% of gas revenue).** Liquid O2/N2/Ar/H2/CO2 trucked from regional ASUs to mid-size customers (food packaging, hospitals, metal-fabricators). 1–5 year contracts with pricing power but more cyclical volume.
3. **Packaged (cylinder) (~28% of gas revenue).** Small-scale users — welding shops, labs, hospitals, small fabricators. Highest gross margin per molecule, lowest absolute scale.

(Plus ~11% in Engineering project revenue, which is lumpy and lower-margin but builds Linde's own asset base.)

The on-site book is the strategic asset. Each new on-site contract signs at returns >10% IRR after-tax and adds to a near-permanent revenue base — the **sale-of-gas backlog** stood at ~$10B+ at end-FY25 (project backlog yet to enter service), with electronics representing the majority. ^[inferred from Investor Day commentary; exact split not disclosed]

### Geographic footprint

Operations in 100+ countries; ~66,000 employees. Density advantages in the US Gulf Coast (largest pipeline H2/N2/O2 grid in the world), Benelux/Rhine-Ruhr, Bavarian chemical triangle, Yangtze River industrial corridor, southern Korea, Taiwan, Singapore. The fab-cluster overlap with TSMC/Samsung/Intel sites is geographic destiny.

## 3. AI buildout exposure

Linde's AI exposure is **less concentrated than COHR or VRT or POWL** but more **durable**. The exposure flows through three channels:

### 3.1 Direct: leading-edge fab on-site contracts (Electronics segment)

This is the marquee channel. A leading-edge logic fab (e.g., TSMC AZ Fab 21) consumes:

- **Bulk N2:** ~3,000–5,000 tons/day per fab module, used for purging, blanketing, and cleanroom positive pressure. Contract life 15–20 years. Pricing typically $80–150/ton plus power pass-through. Lifetime contract value $1.0–1.8B per fab module.
- **Bulk O2:** ~500–1,000 tons/day for oxidation processes.
- **Helium (specialty):** ultra-high-purity He for cryogenic implants, leak-test, and specialty processes. Margin per kg is very high; scarcity is real and Linde controls major helium reserves (Algeria, Qatar, Russia historically — though Russian flow is limited; US BLM supply transition).
- **Specialty / electronic gases:** NF3, WF6, dopants, dilute mixes — these are the highest-margin molecules in the entire gas portfolio. 50%+ gross margin. Linde manufactures NF3 at Wuxi, China and Yongin, Korea with US capacity expanding (announced 2025).
- **Hydrogen:** for purge and reduction steps; also for fuel-cell backup at certain fabs.

Per the Q4 2025 prepared remarks, **Linde has signed on-site supply agreements for "substantially every leading-edge fab announced in the United States."** Specific announced wins (compiled from press releases and project trade press through April 2026):

- **TSMC Arizona (Phoenix):** N2/H2/specialty for Fabs 1, 2, 3 (announced 2022; scope expanded with Fab 3 in 2025).
- **Samsung Taylor, TX:** specialty + bulk; original announcement 2022, scope expansion 2025.
- **Intel Ohio (New Albany):** on-site bulk and specialty (announced 2023; project schedule slipped per Intel Apr 2025 update; gas contract is in place but ramp pushed to 2027–28).
- **Micron Idaho (Boise) and Clay, NY:** on-site agreement in place; Idaho ramps first.
- **TSMC Kumamoto (Japan):** Linde won initial bulk; specialty allocation split with Air Liquide / Nippon Sanso.
- **TSMC Dresden (ESMC JV):** bulk + specialty contract announced 2024.

Each modern leading-edge fab cluster carries $2–5B of lifetime gas-sale revenue at high incremental margins. The 2024–28 wave of global fab announcements has loaded Linde's project backlog with multi-year revenue that is already contracted, partially in construction, and substantially de-risked.

### 3.2 Indirect: hyperscaler campus + power-gen demand

Less material today but a genuine 5-year tailwind:

- **Liquid nitrogen for cooling pilots.** Two-phase / immersion cooling at hyperscaler scale uses N2-based dielectric fluids and liquid-N2-cooled heat exchangers in some pilot designs. Volumes are modest today but growing.
- **Hydrogen for backup power.** Microsoft has piloted PEM fuel-cell backup at Quincy WA, and Equinix has announced a hydrogen pilot at IBX. If even 5% of new hyperscaler backup load shifts to hydrogen by 2030, that's a multi-hundred-million-dollar incremental gas-supply opportunity for Linde.
- **CO2 / inerting / fire suppression.** Battery rooms, switchgear rooms, lithium-ion BESS systems on hyperscaler campuses use clean-agent fire suppression (FM200, NOVEC1230, or CO2). Linde supplies the bulk CO2 plus refill cycles.
- **Industrial-gas demand from new gas-turbine + co-gen power plants serving hyperscaler campuses.** Each new GE Vernova / Mitsubishi gas turbine in the AI-power buildout requires industrial gases for commissioning and operation (N2 for purging, H2 for generator cooling, CO2 for shutdown). Modest per-unit but additive across hundreds of new turbines through 2030.

### 3.3 Hydrogen: optionality on the AI power-mix

The blue/green hydrogen ecosystem matters for AI in two ways: (a) low-carbon hydrogen as feedstock for ammonia → low-carbon power generation; (b) hydrogen for fuel-cell backup. Linde is a top-3 global hydrogen producer with the largest US Gulf Coast pipeline H2 system. Active projects include:

- **OCI Beaumont blue ammonia.** Linde supplies hydrogen via existing GC pipeline. Operational.
- **ExxonMobil Baytown blue hydrogen complex.** 1 Bcf/d capacity; Linde executes engineering and operates the on-site SMR + carbon capture; FID confirmed 2024; first production targeted 2027–28. **This is a big project for Linde — total capex ~$2B+ to Linde's account, with anchor 20-year offtake economics.**
- **Phillips 66 Sweeny.** Smaller but in service.
- **NEOM green-H2 (Saudi).** Air Products is anchor offtaker; Linde participates via Engineering services and is positioned for European import-terminal partnerships. Optionality, not yet revenue.

The 5-year case **does not require hydrogen to be a hit.** But if 2 of the 4 major announced US blue-H2 hubs reach steady-state by 2030, that's incremental $1.5–2.0B of long-duration, contracted revenue at 30%+ adj. EBIT margins.

### 3.4 Quantifying total AI-levered revenue

Combining all three channels for FY25:

| Channel | Estimated AI-levered revenue FY25 | % of consolidated |
|---|---|---|
| Electronics (substantially all is leading-edge / advanced packaging) | ~$3.4B | ~10% |
| Data-center bulk gas, He, fire-suppression, fuel cells | ~$0.5B | ~1.5% |
| Hydrogen (portion AI-power-related) | ~$0.3B | ~1% |
| **Total AI-levered** | **~$4.2B** | **~12.5%** |

(Author estimate; Linde does not disclose this cut. ^[inferred])

By FY30, with Electronics compounding at ~10% and the data-center / hydrogen channels growing faster off a small base, AI-levered revenue could plausibly reach ~$7–9B (~17–20% of consolidated). That is not "Linde becomes an AI company" — it's "Linde retains its compounder profile while adding a meaningful and durable AI tailwind."

This is why LIN's role in an AI portfolio is the **anchor / lower-volatility position** rather than the high-beta bet.

## 4. Demand drivers — what customers are actually saying

### 4.1 Pricing — the perennial driver

Linde delivers ~3% pricing per year, **on average across cycles**. In FY25, price was +3% with positive contribution in all four geographies. This is not promotional pricing — most contracts have CPI escalators or market-based adjusters, plus full power pass-through. Pricing in electronics specialty gases is particularly sticky because qualification cycles (12–24 months) make customer-switching prohibitively expensive.

Q4 2025 call (paraphrased): *"Pricing remained healthy across the portfolio with low-single-digit price contribution everywhere and mid-single-digits in pockets like helium and specialty electronics."*

### 4.2 Volume — slow grind with upside from project backlog

Underlying volume in FY25 was ~+1% Y/Y (Americas +1%, EMEA -1%, APAC +3%). The constructive read is: with industrial activity still mixed, Linde delivered EPS growth almost entirely from price + productivity + buyback. Volume is the **option** if/when global PMI re-accelerates. The project backlog converting from construction to operating revenue adds another 1–2% volume layer through FY27–FY28.

### 4.3 Project backlog — the contracted growth

End-2025 sale-of-gas backlog: ~$7B+ (project value to be commissioned over the next 3–5 years; total project backlog including third-party engineering work ~$10B). This is the most important non-financial KPI for Linde — it's the look-ahead at how much revenue is already under contract but not yet earning.

Major projects in the backlog (as of Q4 2025 / Q1 2026 commentary):

- ExxonMobil Baytown low-carbon H2 complex (Linde investment ~$2B; capacity 1 Bcf/d).
- TSMC Arizona Fab 3 expansion gas package.
- Multiple Korean / Taiwanese leading-edge fab on-site gas.
- China Yangtze pipeline expansion (regional density-build).
- Several Indian electronics + chemicals on-site contracts (smaller individually).

### 4.4 Hyperscaler / data-center signals to watch

- **Helium volumes.** Helium is a leading indicator of leak-test demand at fabs and at advanced data-center cooling pilots. Linde's helium business has tightened since 2022; ASPs have risen materially. Watch helium ASP commentary in segment color.
- **Hydrogen RFQs from hyperscaler-adjacent power-gen.** Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon are all evaluating hydrogen for portions of their backup-power stack. RFQ activity is the leading signal; FID is the lagging signal. Watch trade press (Hydrogen Insight, Power Engineering International).
- **Bulk N2 for cooling.** Watch for explicit data-center N2 references in earnings color — currently bundled into "Other / Manufacturing."

### 4.5 Management framing of the AI tailwind

Sanjiv Lamba on the Q4 2025 call (paraphrased): *"The Electronics end market is one of our most durable growth drivers. We have signed long-term contracts for substantially every leading-edge fab announced in the United States, and we expect electronics sale-of-gas backlog to continue building as customers re-shore. Beyond fabs, we're seeing growing demand from advanced packaging, AI infrastructure, and the broader data-center economy."* ^[paraphrased]

Matt White (CFO) on capital deployment (paraphrased): *"Our project IRR hurdle is unchanged. We are not chasing volume for volume's sake. The backlog is what it is because the customers who want to lock in 15-to-20-year supply agreements are doing so on Linde's terms."* ^[paraphrased]

## 5. Supply / capacity — Linde's industrial assets

### 5.1 Air separation units (ASUs)

Hundreds of ASUs globally; the largest are at Houston Ship Channel, Antwerp, Ulsan, Kashima, and several Yangtze sites. New leading-edge fab on-site ASUs are typically 1,500–3,000 ton/day cryogenic units. Construction lead time 24–36 months; capex $200–500M per ASU. Linde's Engineering segment builds ~80% of these in-house at vendor cost.

### 5.2 Hydrogen production assets

- ~1,000 miles of pipeline H2 (US Gulf Coast — the largest H2 pipeline grid in the world).
- ~70+ steam-methane reformers globally.
- Carbon-capture-equipped SMRs operational at La Porte, TX (Praxair legacy) and being added at Baytown.
- Electrolyzer footprint: smaller (~50 MW operational + ~200 MW under construction across Europe and US); Linde positions itself as the offtake/distribution partner rather than the merchant electrolyzer operator.

### 5.3 Helium reserves

Linde controls helium supply from Algeria (Skikda LNG plant), Qatar (RasGas), Tanzania (Helium One project — long-dated), and US fields (Cliffside, TX BLM legacy + ExxonMobil's Shute Creek WY purification stream). Helium is structurally tight; Linde's reserve position is one of the under-appreciated competitive moats.

### 5.4 Specialty / electronics gas plants

- **NF3 at Wuxi (China)** — largest single-plant capacity globally.
- **NF3 expansion at Yongin (Korea)** — announced 2024.
- **NF3 / WF6 / specialty mix-blending** at multiple US sites; new Texas specialty plant supporting AZ + TX fabs announced 2025.

### 5.5 Capex profile

FY25 capex was ~$5.0B (project + sustaining). Project capex makes up ~$3.0B; sustaining ~$2.0B. The project-capex line is **a function of the backlog signing rate** — if the next 12 months bring 2–3 more major fab on-site contracts plus a hydrogen FID, FY26–FY27 capex steps up to ~$5.5–6.0B/year. Each $1B of project capex generates ~$300M of incremental revenue at 30%+ adj. operating margin once commissioned (3-year lag). This is the math behind the steady ~10–12% return on invested capital.

### 5.6 ROIC and capital discipline

Adj. ROIC FY25: ~25.7%. This is best-in-class for industrials. Linde's discipline on project IRR (>10% after-tax hurdle) plus the buyback floor (~$5–6B/year) keeps incremental capital efficient. Management has repeatedly walked away from projects (notably some hydrogen FIDs in Europe) where customer terms didn't clear the hurdle — that's a positive signal.

### 6.3 Capital allocation

Linde's capital-allocation policy (reaffirmed annually):

1. **Maintain investment-grade balance sheet** (target ~1.5–2.0x net leverage; FY25 actual ~1.6x).
2. **Fund the project backlog at >10% IRR.**
3. **Pay and grow the dividend** (~30 consecutive annual dividend increases — Linde/Praxair lineage; 2025 raise was 8%).
4. **Return excess via buyback** (~$5–6B/year recently; FY25 retired ~1.7% of shares).

There is **no material M&A appetite** in the public commentary — Linde has explicitly said it doesn't see attractive scale acquisitions in industrial gas (Air Liquide is too big, Air Products too dilutive at recent valuations, Nippon Sanso strategic to Asia). This is a **net positive for shareholders**: the buyback floor stays intact.

## 9. Bear case — what would break the thesis

### 9.1 Industrial recession / volume weakness

Global PMI sustained <48 for >12 months would compress Linde's volume to zero or negative across Manufacturing, Metals, and Food. Pricing power partially offsets, but sustained recession is the canonical industrial-gas downside. Estimated bear-case earnings drag: ~6–8% off baseline EPS for two years.

**Mitigant:** the take-or-pay on-site book (~33% of revenue) is volume-insensitive in the short term. Healthcare (~18%) is recession-proof. So the cyclical exposure is roughly 50% of revenue, not 100%.

### 9.2 Hydrogen project execution / FID stall

If Baytown slips materially or comes in over budget, FY28–FY30 revenue takes a hit relative to base case. NEOM has already had timeline slippage; if European green-H2 economics don't pencil at scale, that's an option the market isn't paying for — but it's also not in the base case.

**Mitigant:** Linde's project-IRR discipline means it can walk away from bad hydrogen projects (and has). The downside is FY30 revenue ~$2B lower; the upside (already not in base) is ~$2–4B higher.

### 9.3 Air Liquide pricing competition

In any given on-site tender, Linde is bidding against Air Liquide first and APD/Nippon Sanso second. If Air Liquide turns aggressive (it has at points historically — early 2010s), pricing power softens. The duopoly is rational because both companies have similar capital-allocation discipline; an irrational episode would be a real risk.

**Mitigant:** Air Liquide's own capital-discipline framework mirrors Linde's. Both companies are public, both have committed >10% IRR hurdles, both have shareholder bases that punish deviation. Probability of a sustained price war: low.

### 9.4 Dollar strength / FX translation

LIN reports in USD and ~60% of revenue is non-USD. A sustained dollar rally (DXY +10%) compresses headline revenue and EPS by ~3–5% for a year. This has been an active drag in multiple recent years.

**Mitigant:** management uses ex-FX framing for the underlying performance. Long-term shareholders should look through this; short-term it's a real EPS headwind.

### 9.5 Helium / specialty supply disruption

Geopolitical disruption to Algerian or Qatari helium would tighten supply further (modestly bullish for ASPs but at the cost of volume). A larger structural disruption (e.g., loss of Russian helium permanently — already largely happened) is already priced in.

### 9.6 China industrial slowdown or tariff escalation

APAC is ~21% of revenue. China is the largest piece. Sustained China industrial weakness or US-China trade war escalation that affects the Yangtze pipeline economics or the Wuxi NF3 plant would be a tangible drag.

**Mitigant:** China APAC is well-diversified across coatings, electronics, manufacturing; no single customer concentration; pipeline-network density makes it nearly impossible for a domestic player to dislodge Linde at scale.

### 9.7 ESG / hydrogen-credit mechanism risk

US 45V hydrogen production tax credit rules continue to evolve; if "blue" hydrogen projects lose access to 45V or if 45V is repealed in a future Congress, Baytown economics get worse.

**Mitigant:** 45V structurally favors blue hydrogen (paired with CCS) at lower threshold than green; a repeal would hurt all peers equally; project economics still work without 45V on conservative gas-price assumptions per management commentary. ^[inferred]

### 9.8 Valuation / multiple compression

The biggest risk to a 5-year hold is **time-mean reversion** in the multiple. If LIN re-rates from 26x to 22x over 5 years, that's a ~15% headwind to total return — meaning a 13% EPS CAGR translates to ~10% total return (still acceptable but no longer exceptional).

**Mitigant:** the LIN premium is structural (ROIC, execution, balance-sheet discipline). The main external multiple anchor is the long bond — if 10-year rates spike to 6%+, multiples compress across the entire compounder universe and LIN with them.

### 9.9 What would force me to sell

- Sustained pricing degradation (<2% for two consecutive quarters with no FX or mix explanation).
- Project-IRR discipline visibly slipping (a project announcement at <8% IRR).
- A meaningful (>20%) operating-margin compression that isn't explained by mix or one-offs.
- A change in CEO / CFO that isn't telegraphed and isn't internal.
- Helium or specialty backlog explicitly declining for two quarters.

None of these are visible today.

## 10. Position sizing — role in the AI portfolio

LIN is the **anchor compounder** position. Characteristics:

- **Mega-cap** ($224B). Liquidity is not a concern.
- **Beta ~0.75** (5-year). Lowest-vol position in the AI basket.
- **Earnings predictability:** zero misses since the 2018 merger; guidance achievement near 100%.
- **Dividend yield ~1.25%** with ~7–9% annual growth; dividend reinvested adds ~1 pp to the IRR.
- **Buyback floor** ~$5–6B/year structurally retires ~2% of shares annually.
- **Pairs well with:** higher-beta AI plays (COHR, VRT, ETN, POWL). LIN provides ballast; the high-beta names provide upside.
- **Suggested weight in an AI basket:** 8–15%. Larger if the portfolio overall is high-beta.
- **Pairs trade idea:** long LIN / short APD as the relative-quality expression of the industrial-gas duopoly. Not recommended here as we're sizing the absolute long.
- **Comparison to other anchor compounders in this wiki's AI universe:** LIN's profile sits between ETN (electrical equipment compounder) and JCI (HVAC compounder) — comparable beta (~0.7–0.9), comparable EPS-CAGR algorithm (low double-digits), but LIN has the highest contract-duration / take-or-pay component, which is the most differentiated risk-mitigant.

**Specific portfolio role:** the position the portfolio holds across cycles. You don't trade LIN. You let it compound. The dividend is paid; the buyback retires shares; the backlog converts to revenue; the EPS grows; the multiple may compress modestly but the absolute earnings power keeps stepping. Five years of that is the bet.
