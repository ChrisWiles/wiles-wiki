## 1. One-paragraph thesis

Quanta is the largest pure-play utility EPC in North America and the *only* contractor with the labor density to absorb the simultaneous transmission, distribution, substation, renewables, and hyperscaler-campus build cycles. Backlog hit a record **$48.5B** in Q1 2026 (RPO $26.2B), up from $44.0B at YE2025 and $34.5B at YE2024. ^[extracted] Q1 2026 revenue grew **26.3% YoY to $7.87B**, adjusted EPS jumped **51% to $2.68**, and management raised FY2026 to $34.7-$35.2B revenue / $13.55-$14.25 adj. EPS — implying ~26% adj-EPS growth on top of the 20% the company already printed in 2025. ^[extracted] Five proprietary advantages compound: (1) ~69,500 craft-skilled employees and ownership of Northwest Lineman College — the binding constraint on every utility's capex plan; (2) the **Cupertino Electric** acquisition (closed Jul-2024 at $1.54B) which gave Quanta the data-center substation/whitespace business at exactly the right moment; (3) growing share of programmatic / multi-year MSA contracts that lock in margin; (4) a vertical-integration play into 345-765 kV transformer and breaker manufacturing ($500-$700M capex over several years) that hedges supply-chain exposure for Quanta's own jobs; (5) a bolt-on M&A engine — eight deals in 2025 including three in Q4 totaling $1.7B — that adds margin-accretive specialty workforces faster than organic hiring could. Through-cycle Electric Infrastructure margin should walk from ~10.7% (2025) toward 11.5-12.5% (2027-2028) as data-center, storm-hardening, and FERC-Order-2023-driven transmission work shifts the mix. The 12-month case is straightforward: management beat-and-raised every quarter of 2025 and just did it again on 2026-04-30; Citi went to $733 PT 27-Apr; Stifel and Bernstein raised after Q4. ^[extracted] At ~31x LTM EV/EBITDA / ~26x forward, PWR is not cheap, but neither is the demand curve; it is one of a small handful of names whose backlog and labor moat let it grow into a premium multiple rather than de-rate out of one.

## 2. Business overview

Quanta is, in a single line, the company a US utility calls when it needs to **string a 500 kV transmission line through the Permian, restore service after a hurricane in Florida, build a substation for an Ashburn data center, lay a 36-inch gas distribution line in Pennsylvania, and operate the largest accredited lineman college in North America** — sometimes all in the same week. The business is built around 200+ regional operating subsidiaries that share corporate safety, fleet, training, bonding, and capital allocation but operate locally. The decentralized structure is what lets Quanta self-perform on jobs that a national-only competitor would have to subcontract.

Through Q4 2025 Quanta reported **three** segments. Starting Q1 2026 the company collapsed reporting into **two** segments — "Electric Infrastructure Solutions" (the historical Electric Power + Renewable Energy lines) and "Underground Utility and Infrastructure Solutions" — to reflect how customers actually buy. ^[extracted] For modeling I keep three economic buckets:

| Segment (economic) | FY2025 revenue ^[extracted] | FY2025 op-margin ^[extracted] | What's inside |
|---|---|---|---|
| Electric Power Infrastructure | ~$18.0B | ~11.0% | Transmission lines (115-765 kV), substations, distribution, storm restoration, EV/grid mod, technology-driven load (data centers, semis, manufacturing) — Cupertino Electric is housed here. |
| Renewable Energy Infrastructure | ~$5.6B | ~7.5-8.5% | Utility-scale solar EPC (Blattner), wind, BESS. Margin lower because of project-based exposure and historical solar panel/IRA noise. |
| Underground / Infrastructure | ~$4.9B | ~7.5-8% | Gas utility distribution, large-diameter transmission pipeline, civil/mechanical (DSI acquired Jul-2025), industrial. |
| **Total** | **~$28.5B** | **~10.0% adj-EBITDA** ^[extracted: $2.9B FY25] | |

Customer mix: investor-owned utilities are still the plurality (~50%+ of revenue), but the share of "technology and load center" customers (hyperscalers, semi fabs, advanced-manufacturing facilities) is the fastest-growing slice — management guided Q4 2025 to end-market growth of **60-80% in 2026** for technology/load centers, vs. 10-20% for grid and gas, and 10-15% for power generation. ^[extracted] Cupertino Electric, the data-center contractor Quanta bought in July 2024 for $1.54B ($1.3B cash + $225M stock + up to $200M earnout), contributed approximately $2.3-$2.4B in revenue in 2025 ^[extracted] and is on track to be a $3B+ business in 2026.

The decentralized operating model (200+ subsidiaries) is genuinely a moat: each unit is regional and customer-embedded, but Quanta corporate sets safety, training, fleet, capital allocation, and bonding capacity. Competitors who try to replicate this either lack the labor (EME's IES is more buildings-focused) or lack the utility scale (MTZ is broader telecom/clean-energy, MYRG is one-tenth the size). Foreign competitors (China State Construction, etc.) are not procurement-eligible for North American utility transmission for security and labor-law reasons.

## 3. AI buildout exposure — quantified

Direct AI exposure runs through three channels:

1. **Hyperscaler campus EPC (Cupertino).** Cupertino's pre-acquisition revenue was ~$2.5B with the bulk in Bay Area / hyperscale technology. Inside Quanta this folds into Electric Infrastructure. Management said on Q1 2026 that "data center and large load customers represent the fastest-growing segment of backlog" and that they see daily inbound RFPs for substation + whitespace + medium-voltage build. ^[extracted] My estimate: pro-forma data-center / hyperscaler-direct revenue is **$3.0-$3.5B in 2026 (~9-10% of group)**, growing 30-50% in 2026-2027.
2. **Utility transmission upgrades to feed AI load.** Indirect but bigger. PJM, ERCOT, MISO and SPP are filing IRPs that require **multi-hundred-circuit-mile** new 345-765 kV transmission to move generation to load centers; FERC Order 2023's cluster-study rules and the DOE-directed FERC ANOPR on large-load interconnection (deadline 2026-04-30) ^[extracted] both push the transmission queue. Quanta is the dominant North American transmission contractor; no peer can self-perform a 100+ mile, 500-765 kV line at the same speed. I size AI-driven incremental transmission EPC at **$2-$4B/yr for Quanta by 2027-2028** above the baseline, on top of regular reliability spend.
3. **Distribution and substation work for utilities serving data centers.** Smaller per project but higher frequency, captured by the Electric Infrastructure margin mix shifting up.

Combined, I model 2026 "AI-attributable" revenue at **~25-30% of group**, rising to **~35-40% by 2028** as transmission projects move from study to construction.

Management quote, Q1 2026 call (paraphrased from transcript summary): "We're seeing growth not just in data centers, but also in onshoring of chips and robotics, which is driving load and supporting infrastructure needs. Customers are increasingly contracting on a programmatic, multi-year basis." ^[extracted] Q4 2025 call quote framing: data-center end-market growth at **60-80%** in 2026, vs. mid-teens for the rest of the book. ^[extracted] Q3 2025 call: "Daily inbound opportunities in data centers, transmission, and generation" — a hand-wave, but a meaningful one given how careful Quanta's IR is about over-promising. ^[extracted]

What it would take for the AI-attributable share to disappoint: hyperscaler **interconnection slips** (the single binding constraint right now per Wood Mac data: only ~183 GW of ~600 GW of planned campus capacity has secured utility supply ^[extracted from electrical-equipment deep dive]) push campus startups out of 2026-2027 into 2028-2029, smearing revenue. Quanta's actual pain in that scenario is small — they get paid for the work — but the *growth rate* compresses, and that is what the multiple is paying for.

### MSA / programmatic share

Management has been explicit: more revenue is moving to **negotiated, programmatic** multi-year master service agreements — multi-year unit-price work for utilities and hyperscalers — and away from one-off competitive bids. Programmatic share rose into the high-50s % in 2025 from low-50s in 2023. ^[inferred] This is the structural margin lever: MSA work is repeat, well-priced, and reduces the white-knuckle risk of large fixed-price misses.

### Renewables

The Renewable Energy segment had a difficult 2024-2025 (margin in the 6-8% range from solar module timing and project execution noise). 2026 should be the cleanup year: large solar EPC margins are normalizing, BESS attach is rising, and the IRA tax-credit policy backdrop has stabilized after the early-2026 administration noise (Quanta has guided that **a substantial majority** of its renewables backlog is contracted with safe-harbor or already-monetized credits — exposure to retroactive policy reversal is muted but not zero).

### Multi-year MSAs that anchor the book

Quanta does not disclose contract values per customer, but call commentary points to multi-year MSAs in place with:

- The major IOU systems in Texas, the Carolinas, the Southeast (Southern Company), the Mid-Atlantic, and the upper Midwest
- Multiple Canadian utilities (Hydro One; legacy Quanta Canada relationships)
- A growing number of hyperscalers via Cupertino — the post-deal arrangement renews these on multi-year terms rather than spot RFP
- Two major North American gas utilities for distribution integrity work (post-PHMSA mega-rule cycle)

These MSAs are why backlog is not a one-time spike. Replenishment is structural.

## 5. Supply / capacity — the labor moat

The asset is people, and Quanta has scaled the asset:

- **~69,500 employees at YE2025**, up from ~58,400 at YE2024. Eight 2025 acquisitions added approximately **11,100 employees** of which the bulk were craft (linemen, electricians, equipment operators, civil crews). ^[extracted]
- **Northwest Lineman College + Quanta Advanced Training Center** — owned, accredited training pipelines that produce the company's own apprentices. The single most valuable non-balance-sheet asset. Lineman college throughput is roughly 1,000-1,500 graduates per year; the broader Quanta training system produces several thousand new craft hires annually. The fact that Quanta *owns* the college (not just sponsors it) means they get first call on graduates.
- **2025 acquisitions:** Tri-City, Wilson, Billings, DSI (Dynamic Systems — turnkey mechanical/plumbing/process, July 2025), plus the three Q4 deals totaling $1.7B (Cupertino-adjacent and underground). ^[extracted] The pattern: each deal brings 500-2,000 craft workers and a regional / specialty book of business that would have taken Quanta 3-5 years to grow organically.
- **Vertical integration into equipment.** Q4 2025 management announced plans to invest **$500-$700M over several years** to build manufacturing for **345-765 kV transformers and breakers** — capacity to feed Quanta's own large transmission projects, where 2-3 year lead times on EHV gear is currently the binding bottleneck. **2026 capex assumes $250-$350M** of this. ^[extracted] This is identical in motivation to GE Vernova's Prolec roll-up: bring scarce equipment in-house. The economics: if Quanta self-supplies even 20% of its own EHV transformer needs by 2028-2029 at a 15-20% manufacturing margin, that captures $50-$100M of EBITDA that otherwise leaves the contracting envelope, and gives Quanta a 6-12 month schedule advantage on bidding utility work.
- **Why the labor scarcity is durable.** A journeyman lineman is a 4-7 year apprenticeship credential. The North American apprentice pipeline graduates roughly 5,000-7,000 per year and is hiring against a backdrop of: utility T&D capex up ~50% over five years, semi-fab construction (Intel Ohio, TSMC Arizona, Micron NY), reshoring industrial loads, hyperscale campus build, hurricane storm-restoration demand. There is no path for any new entrant to assemble Quanta's craft headcount inside ten years. Even buying competitors only redistributes existing labor.

### Drivers in the base case

- **Revenue growth ramp:** 23% in 2026 (high end of guide + acquisitions), decelerating to ~10% by 2030 as the law of large numbers kicks in. Revenue more-than-doubles 2025-2030.
- **Margin walk:** Adj-EBITDA from 10.1% (2026) to 12.0% (2030) — a 190 bps expansion driven by (a) MSA mix, (b) Cupertino margin normalizing into the low-teens vs. high-single-digits at acquisition, (c) lower share of fixed-price renewables, (d) Quanta-built EHV equipment margin capture, (e) operating leverage on the corporate cost base.
- **EPS growth:** ~26% YoY 2026, average ~17% per year 2026-2030. The largest single delta vs. EBITDA is share-count creep from acquisitions (most are stock-and-cash; Cupertino was 883k shares; future bolt-ons will be similar) — I assume share count drifts to ~155M by 2028 and ~158M by 2030, partially offset by buybacks.
- **FCF:** $1.85B in 2026 (matching guide midpoint), $4.1B by 2030. FCF growth lags EBITDA growth in 2026-2027 because of the EHV mfg capex investment ($250-$350M annually for the first few years).

### Working-capital sensitivity

Quanta's project businesses run **negative-to-mildly-positive** working capital. The Q1 2026 cash flow swing was characteristically heavy — Q1 is historically the weakest cash quarter as crews mobilize. The ~$1.7B FY25 FCF / $1.8B FY26 guide implies a clean FCF-to-EBITDA conversion of **~50-55%** through-cycle, lower than EMCOR (~75%) because Quanta is more contract-billed than time-and-materials. A 200 bps slip in DSO on a $35B revenue base is a ~$190M working-capital drain — meaningful but not thesis-breaking.

The risk to watch is a **construction-in-progress build-up on long-cycle transmission jobs**: Quanta has flagged on Q3-Q4 2025 calls that long-duration EHV projects book inventory ahead of milestones. If the EHV manufacturing investment ($500-$700M capex) is funded out of cash, FCF could be **$1.6-$1.7B in 2026-2027** rather than $1.8-$2.0B at the high end of the guide.

## 7. Valuation

### Backlog × through-cycle margin × multiple

Backlog approach: $48.5B Q1 2026 backlog × ~11.5% through-cycle EBITDA margin = ~$5.6B run-rate EBITDA on the book today. At a **15-17x EBITDA** multiple (where utility-EPC + AI-tied infrastructure compounders trade), that's $84-$95B EV. Net debt is roughly $4-5B (Cupertino + recent deals), so equity value ~$80-$90B. At 150M shares, that's ~$535-$600/share **on backlog alone, with no growth credit**. The market is paying for the FY27-FY28 backlog continuation, not the current book.

## 9. Bear case — what breaks the thesis

1. **Margin mean-reversion.** If Electric Infrastructure margin slips back toward 10.0-10.5% (because Cupertino integration disappoints or large-diameter pipeline drags Underground further), FY27 EBITDA goes to $3.9-$4.1B not $4.50B. The forward multiple compresses; ~$430-$470 fair value.
2. **Renewables tax-credit policy reversal.** Most of the renewable backlog is safe-harbored, but a hostile post-2026 election outcome that retroactively limits IRA credits could reduce 2027-2028 utility-solar EPC bookings 20-40%. Approximate revenue hit: $1.0-$1.5B/yr.
3. **Labor-cost inflation.** The asset is people; if union wage settlements run 6-8% (vs. utility rate increases of 4-5%), the labor-cost gap is absorbed by Quanta's gross margin. Each 100 bps wage outpace = ~50-70 bps EBITDA margin compression. Two years of this and the through-cycle 11.5% margin assumption is wrong.
4. **Large project execution slip.** Transmission EPC has a long history of project blow-ups (Quanta itself has had them — 2017-2019 Latin America was the canonical example). A single $500M-$1B transmission line slipping a year and absorbing $100M of unplanned cost is enough to break a quarter.
5. **M&A integration risk.** Cupertino is the bull case's centerpiece. If the **earnout** ($200M potential, ending in the post-acquisition window) ^[extracted] indicates underperformance, or if Cupertino's data-center customer concentration (it was historically heavily Apple/hyperscaler-Bay-Area) flexes against Quanta on pricing, the segment uplift narrative weakens.
6. **Hyperscaler capex reset.** The whole AI-buildout thesis ([[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]]) is contingent on hyperscaler capex compounding through 2028. A coordinated capex pause — driven by training-compute returns disappointment, an "AI capex digestion" narrative, or a recession — pulls 5-10% of revenue out of the bull-case 2027-2028 numbers in a single quarter of guide cuts. Quanta is less direct than NVIDIA but more direct than utilities; it would de-rate sharply.
7. **Multiple compression from cycle peak.** Even with no operational miss, a return to a ~16-18x forward EBITDA multiple (the 5-year median before the AI-infra re-rating) on flat 2027 numbers is ~$430. **The largest single risk is multiple, not earnings.**

## 10. Position-sizing and correlation

- **Beta ~1.0** (5-year) and ~1.1 (1-year) — large-cap industrial, moves with cyclicals more than tech.
- **Correlation with EME ~0.7, MTZ ~0.6, FIX ~0.65** — meaningfully correlated; do not treat PWR + EME + FIX as a diversified basket. They will all draw down together in a hyperscaler-capex scare.
- **Correlation with NVDA / hyperscaler basket ~0.4-0.5** — second-order, not tier-1.
- **Liquidity:** $1.5-$2.0B average daily traded; institutional float deep; not a sizing constraint.
- **Suggested role in the portfolio:** core compounder in the second-order AI-infra bucket. **5-10% of an AI-infra sleeve, max 3-5% of total portfolio.** Pair with FIX (mechanical), with electrical equipment OEMs (ETN, GEV — see [[electrical-equipment-deep-dive]]), and with one utility for the actual ratepayer side. Do not double up by also owning MTZ/STRL/EME at full sizing — pick one or two from the EPC bucket.
- **Trim trigger:** if forward EV/EBITDA exceeds ~28x without backlog growth, or if quarterly book-to-bill drops below 1.0x for two consecutive quarters.
- **Add trigger:** any 15%+ drawdown that does not coincide with a guide cut (i.e., multiple compression alone) is a buy.

## 12. What would change my mind

**Upgrade to "very high conviction" (and overweight further):**
- Two consecutive quarters of Electric Infrastructure operating margin >12%
- Backlog growth re-accelerates above +35% YoY (vs. current +37% Q1, decelerating in 2H assumption)
- A second large data-center-tied platform acquisition (a "Cupertino East") executed at <12x EBITDA
- DOE/FERC large-load interconnection rule lands cleanly and unblocks PJM queue meaningfully

- A single fixed-price project blow-up that is *systemic* rather than idiosyncratic — i.e., where the post-mortem reveals bid discipline issues
- A YoY backlog growth print below +15% (not absolute decline — just deceleration enough to puncture the compounding narrative)
- Hyperscaler capex guide cuts (NVDA/MSFT/META/GOOG capex commentary ticks meaningfully lower)
- Cupertino earnout misses materially (signals data-center revenue / margin underperformance)

**Sell:**
- Multiple compression to <14x forward EBITDA without an offsetting backlog catalyst
- Management departure (the CEO, CFO, or the CEO of the renamed Electric Infrastructure segment) without a credible internal successor
- A confirmed delay/cancellation of multiple SunZia-class projects from the construction pipeline
