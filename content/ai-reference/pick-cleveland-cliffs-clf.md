## 1. Thesis in one paragraph

Cleveland-Cliffs is the **only domestic producer of grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES)** in the United States. ^[extracted] Every utility-scale transformer wound in North America that uses American GOES is wound with CLF steel — and the National Infrastructure Advisory Council has formally flagged the LPT/transformer shortage as a US grid-reliability issue, which is locking in federal procurement preference for domestic content. ^[extracted] CLF is doing two things at once: (1) expanding GOES capacity and quality at **Butler Works (PA)** via a $195M project (with $75M DOE grant) on schedule for mid-2028 ^[extracted], and (2) standing up its **own** distribution-transformer assembly plant at the idled **Weirton (WV)** tinplate site for $150M, expected online 1H 2026 ^[extracted]. The Weirton transformer plant is *vertically integrated downstream* of CLF's GOES — it both consumes Butler GOES and earns the OEM-margin layer above the steel. Layered on top: Q2 2026 should mark the first profitable quarter post-cycle, helped by a ~$500M EBITDA tailwind from terminating a loss-making slab contract ^[extracted], rising HRC prices ($980/ton Q1 vs. ~$770 in Q1 2025), tightening Section 232/301 tariff enforcement, and an auto reshoring tailwind ^[extracted]. Twelve-month, base-case fair value sits in the **$13-$15** range, ~30-50% above spot, with a fat-tailed upside scenario into the high-teens if HRC sustains $1,000+ and the GOES order book gets re-priced. The downside is real: HRC mean-reverts, auto wobbles, and net debt is ~$7.3B against an Adj. EBITDA base that printed only $37M for FY2025 ^[extracted], so this name is closer to a "broken cyclical with a structural option" than a clean structural compounder.

**Conviction: HIGH on direction, MEDIUM on magnitude.** The structural GOES leg is high-conviction; the 12-month return is gated by macro/HRC.

## 2. Business overview

### 2.1 What CLF actually is

After the 2020 acquisition of AK Steel, the 2020 acquisition of ArcelorMittal USA, and the 2024 acquisition of Canadian flat-rolled producer **Stelco** (~$2.5B EV, closed Oct 2024) ^[extracted], CLF is the **largest flat-rolled steel producer in North America**, with the integrated iron-ore-to-finished-coil footprint that no Chinese or Korean import can credibly replicate. The 2025 attempted acquisition of US Steel was blocked when Nippon Steel's $14.1B bid prevailed and closed in June 2025 with the "golden share" national-security agreement ^[extracted] — leaving CLF as the *un-foreign-controlled* domestic flat-roll champion, which the Trump administration's Buy-American framework is actively favoring in federal procurement (see the $400M DLA defense GOES contract, below). ^[extracted]

### 2.2 Segments and product mix

CLF reports two segments:

| Segment | What it is | % of revenue (FY25) ^[inferred] | Why it matters here |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Steelmaking** | HRC, CRC, coated, plate, electrical steel (GOES + NOES), tinplate, slab | ~98% | Where the cyclical core lives; also where GOES sits |
| **Other Operations** | Iron ore for third parties, pellets, mining services, scrap | ~2% | Mostly internal |

Within Steelmaking, the customer mix in Q4 2025 was disclosed as **~28% direct automotive** (~$1.1B of ~$3.9B quarterly steelmaking revenue) ^[extracted], with the remainder split across infrastructure, construction, energy/electrical (where GOES lives), service centers, and converters. Auto is the single biggest end-market and the reason CLF's beta is structurally higher than Nucor's.

### 2.3 The footprint

CLF's flat-roll production base is concentrated in the Great Lakes:

- **Burns Harbor (IN)** — large integrated mill, blast furnace + BOF
- **Indiana Harbor (IN)** — largest integrated mill in North America
- **Cleveland (OH)** — integrated, blast furnace
- **Middletown (OH)** — integrated; getting upgraded blast-furnace configuration ^[extracted]
- **Dearborn (MI)** — integrated, auto-heavy
- **Stelco (Hamilton/Lake Erie ON)** — Canadian flat-roll, doubles spot-market exposure ^[extracted]
- **Butler Works (PA)** — **GOES + NOES electrical steel**, the only US GOES producer; $195M expansion in flight ^[extracted]
- **Zanesville (OH)** — finishing for electrical steel
- **Weirton (WV)** — formerly tinplate, idled 2024, restarting 2026 as **transformer assembly** ($150M, 600 USW jobs) ^[extracted]
- Plus mini-mill / coating / finishing facilities and the iron-ore mining base in Minnesota and Michigan

The footprint is essentially un-replicable in any reasonable timeframe — that is the moat. The cyclical question is what the through-cycle margin is on top of that footprint.

## 3. The thesis legs in detail

### 3.1 GOES monopoly: the only US producer

GOES — grain-oriented electrical steel — is the magnetic-core material in every power transformer and most distribution transformers. NOES — non-oriented electrical steel — goes into motors and generators. The global producer set is small (~6 producers globally: CLF, Nippon Steel, JFE, POSCO, NLMK, ThyssenKrupp), and **CLF is the only producer in the United States**. ^[extracted]

Why is this a real moat?

1. **Capital intensity.** A new GOES line requires cold-rolling, decarburization, secondary-recrystallization annealing, and insulation coating — a 4-5 year capex-to-first-coil project. ^[inferred]
2. **Yield knowledge.** GOES grades are defined by core loss at specific frequencies and inductions; getting Hi-B / domain-refined grades to spec requires accumulated process knowledge. ^[inferred]
3. **Buy-American + Section 232 tariffs.** Section 232 25% steel tariffs apply to imported GOES. Section 301 anti-dumping orders on Chinese, Russian, and Korean GOES have effectively walled off cheap import competition. ^[extracted]
4. **NIAC reliability designation.** The 2024 NIAC report on LPT scarcity gives CLF's GOES customers (transformer OEMs) a regulatory tailwind to procure domestic GOES even at a premium. ^[extracted]

The DLA awarded CLF a **$400M, 5-year, 53,000-ton GOES contract** (Defense Logistics Agency) for transformer and generator steel. ^[extracted] At ~10,600 tons/year, this is a high-margin anchor order that locks in capacity utilization through ~2030.

GOES pricing has reportedly **doubled since 2020** ^[extracted]. Management on the Q1 2026 call reaffirmed they are not just the only producer but "the ones that are growing production with our project in Butler" ^[extracted] — explicitly framing GOES as a market they intend to lean into.

### 3.2 Butler Works expansion ($195M, mid-2028)

In December 2025, CLF presented a $195M expansion plan for Butler Works (PA) ^[extracted]; in April 2026 (just before this dossier's date), the Butler township board granted full approval. ^[extracted] The expansion adds **four induction reheat furnaces**, replaces two natural-gas furnaces, and is funded in part by a **$75M DOE Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations grant** ^[extracted]. Operational target is **July/August 2028**. ^[extracted]

The expansion is *quality* and *throughput*, not just throughput — the induction furnaces are intended to improve consistency of Hi-B grades, which is exactly what the data-center-driven LPT market needs (lower core losses, lower noise, higher allowed flux density). The Butler expansion is the supply answer to the demand thesis in [[electrical-equipment-deep-dive]]: GOES queues currently extend into 2027 ^[inferred], and Butler comes online in mid-2028 to backfill the next leg of demand growth.

The 2028 timing means **Butler is not a 12-month catalyst directly** — but the *contract pipeline* the expansion supports is signed in 2026-2027 and that's what re-rates the stock.

### 3.3 Weirton transformer plant ($150M, online 1H 2026)

This is the under-appreciated leg. CLF is repurposing the idled Weirton (WV) tinplate facility into a **distribution transformer assembly plant** ^[extracted]. Key facts:

- **$150M total capex**, with $50M of that as a forgivable loan from West Virginia. ^[extracted]
- **600 USW-represented jobs reactivated** at a site that was indefinitely idled in 2024. ^[extracted]
- Target: **three-phase distribution transformers** for utilities and large commercial / data center applications.
- Schedule: GOES coil production already restarted late 2025; finished-transformer assembly **online 1H 2026**. ^[extracted]
- Strategic logic: vertical integration. CLF *consumes its own GOES* at Weirton, capturing the transformer-OEM gross margin layer (typically 15-25% on distribution transformers vs. ~5-10% steel-mill GP) on top of the GOES sale. ^[inferred]

The **derivative-products tariff inclusion** is critical: distribution transformers were added to the Section 232 derivative-products list in 2025-2026, which management on the Q1 2026 call explicitly called out as "exactly the right outcome." ^[extracted] This raises the price ceiling of imported transformers (Mexican, Korean, Chinese, Indian assemblers) and protects the unit economics at Weirton.

The Weirton ramp is the **most direct 12-month catalyst** in the thesis. If CLF can demonstrate first-shipment plus a credible 2026 exit-rate run-rate by Q3-Q4 2026 prints, the stock should re-rate on the GOES + transformer sum-of-parts.

### 3.4 Transformer-OEM customer relationships

CLF's GOES customers are the transformer OEMs covered in [[electrical-equipment-deep-dive]]:

- **Hitachi Energy** — building South Boston VA LPT plant ($457M, online 2028)
- **GE Vernova / Prolec** — completed Prolec acquisition; 5 GW Prolec transformer deal with ON.energy for AI DC ^[extracted]
- **Siemens Energy** — North Carolina expansion
- **Eaton (ETN)** — $340M Jonesville SC three-phase transformer plant ^[extracted]
- **Mitsubishi Electric (MEPPI)** — $86M Western PA transformer plant
- Plus ERMCO, Howard, Hammond, WEG, SPX

Every one of those plants needs GOES coils. Most of those plants are inside the US tariff perimeter, which means **they buy from CLF or from Nippon/JFE/POSCO at a Section-232-loaded price**. CLF's economics are set by the marginal imported GOES price net of tariff, which gives them substantial pricing power.

### 3.5 Auto reshoring secondary upside

CLF's largest end-market is automotive (~28% of steelmaking revenue). ^[extracted] Two parallel tailwinds:

1. **Auto cyclical recovery.** 2025 auto production was below normalized run-rate; the 2026 setup includes inventory restocking and tariff-driven OEM reshoring of production from Mexico to the US. CLF management on the Q4 2025 call said they "have already secured more business from automotive clients" for 2026. ^[extracted]
2. **EV body and motor steel mix.** EV bodies need higher-strength steels and motors need NOES — both higher-margin product than commodity HRC. CLF's Cleveland and Dearborn facilities are auto-qualified, and the Stelco acquisition added Canadian capacity that auto OEMs prefer for nearshoring.

This is the **leveraged option**: if 2026 SAAR finishes >16M and Big-3 reshoring continues, auto realized prices and mix lift CLF's blended ASP by $30-60/ton above HRC index. ^[inferred]

### 3.6 The slab-contract termination

The biggest *one-time* tailwind to 2026 EBITDA is the termination of a long-running, loss-making slab contract (with what was historically ArcelorMittal USA, structurally inherited as part of the 2020 ArcelorMittal acquisition). Management has guided to a **~$500M EBITDA boost** from the termination ^[extracted], with 175,000 tons of slab still in the tail of shipments as of Q1. ^[extracted] This is real money — CLF FY2025 Adj. EBITDA was only **$37M** ^[extracted], so $500M is more than 13× the trailing base. It's also somewhat under-modeled by the sell-side, which is part of why we think the bear-case dispersion (GLJ at $9.42) is too tight.

## 4. AI buildout exposure

### 4.1 Indirect but levered

CLF doesn't sell to hyperscalers. CLF sells GOES to transformer OEMs, who sell transformers to utilities, EPCs, and (in growing share) directly to data-center developers. The exposure chain:

```
AI data center demand
  → utility / DC substation builds
    → transformer OEM order book (HE, GEV/Prolec, ETN, SE, MEPPI, ERMCO, Howard)
      → GOES coil purchases from CLF (Butler) [direct]
      → distribution transformer assembly at CLF Weirton [direct, after 2026]
```

So CLF is a **second-order beneficiary** (see [[second-order-beneficiaries-ranked]]) of AI data-center capex — but it's a *concentrated* second-order play because CLF is the *only* domestic GOES producer and one of only a handful of GOES producers globally.

### 4.2 Sizing the GOES revenue inside CLF

CLF has historically not broken out GOES revenue separately. Public clues:

- The DLA contract is $400M / 53,000 tons = ~$7,500/ton ^[extracted]. That's ~3-4× HRC pricing per ton.
- Public estimates put US GOES consumption at ~150,000-250,000 tpy ^[ambiguous], of which CLF supplies essentially all the domestic-content demand and a portion of the rest at tariff-loaded prices.
- Estimated CLF GOES annual revenue today: **~$0.8B-$1.5B** ^[ambiguous], or **4-8% of total revenue** but plausibly **15-30% of EBITDA at structural margins** ^[ambiguous]. Mark this hard — segment-level GOES margin is not disclosed.

The asymmetry: GOES is a small share of revenue but a large share of *upside in EBITDA*, because steel-mill incremental margin on a tariff-protected specialty grade is dramatically higher than on HRC. The Butler expansion and Weirton ramp re-base both the volume and the margin-per-ton on this part of the business.

### 4.3 Why the AI link is durable, not cyclical

The bull case for CLF's GOES exposure rests on the same arguments as the rest of the [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]]:

1. The transformer shortage is structural (3-5 year capacity-add cycle).
2. NIAC has flagged it as a national reliability issue, locking in policy support.
3. China/Russia/Korea GOES is walled off by Section 232/301.
4. The Wood Mac forecast puts US data-center electrical-equipment TAM at ~$65B by 2030, a 3× from 2025.

The cyclical part of CLF's business (auto, construction, generic flat-roll) is the larger share today. The *structural* part (GOES + Weirton transformers) is small today but compounds at a much higher rate and at a much higher margin. Over 12 months, the cyclical part dominates the print; over 36 months, the structural part dominates the multiple.

## 5. Demand drivers and macro setup

### 5.1 GOES order book

Management commentary across Q3 2025, Q4 2025, and Q1 2026 calls has been consistently constructive on the GOES order book ^[extracted]. Specifically:

- **Domestic transformer OEMs are scrambling for GOES allocations.** CLF is reportedly allocating volume into 2027.
- **DLA $400M anchor** locks in capacity through 2030.
- **Distribution transformer derivative tariff** raised the price ceiling for Weirton output.
- Butler expansion adds capacity and quality (Hi-B grades) into 2028, supporting the next leg of GOES contract pricing.

### 5.2 Tariff policy posture

The Trump 2.0 administration is keeping Section 232 at 25% on steel and selectively expanding the **derivative products** list. ^[extracted] In 2026 Q1:

- Distribution transformers added to derivative products.
- Continued enforcement on Chinese, Russian, Korean, and Vietnamese transhipped steel.
- Buy-American preferences strengthened in federal procurement.

This is essentially the maximum-case tariff regime for CLF. **The risk is reversal** (see Bear case), not further tightening.

### 5.3 HRC pricing direction

Q1 2026 average domestic HRC was **$980/ton**, up 24% YoY ^[extracted]. Management expects ~$60/ton increase in realized prices vs. 2025 ^[extracted]. Industry consensus has HRC sitting in a **$900-1,050/ton range** for FY26 ^[ambiguous], with downside if auto demand softens and upside if tariffs on Mexican/Brazilian slab tighten further. The cash-cost setup matters: integrated producers like CLF break even around $700-750/ton; mini-mills (NUE, STLD) break even around $600-650. So at $980 HRC, CLF generates substantial cash margin per ton; at $700 HRC, CLF is losing money.

### 5.4 45X and IRA-derived credits

CLF is investigating Section 45X advanced-manufacturing tax credits for electrical-steel components used in transformers ^[ambiguous] — this is contested ground because GOES isn't a final clean-energy component, but transformers feeding renewable interconnection arguably qualify. The DOE Butler grant ($75M) is **separate** and confirmed. ^[extracted]

### 6.4 Competitive landscape post-Nippon/USS

The 2025 Nippon-USS close changed the structure:

- **CLF** — only un-foreign-controlled major US flat-roll integrated producer; preferred by Buy-American procurement.
- **USS (Nippon-owned)** — constrained by golden-share NSA, capped on price aggression and on shutting US plants. Competitive pricing posture is *less* aggressive post-deal, not more. ^[inferred]
- **Nucor (NUE)** — mini-mill leader; lower cost; less GOES exposure; bigger in plate and bar.
- **Steel Dynamics (STLD)** — mini-mill; aluminum and Sinton expansion; less integrated.
- **Algoma, Stelco** — Stelco now CLF's; Algoma has ongoing financial stress.

Net for CLF: **the elimination of USS as an aggressive standalone competitor is mildly bullish** for HRC pricing discipline, and structurally bullish for federal procurement market share (DLA, GSA, DOD, DOT). ^[inferred]

## 10. Bear case (do not skip)

This name is genuinely volatile. The bear cases:

### 10.1 HRC cyclical roll (highest-probability bear)

If 2H 2026 sees auto demand soften (rate-driven SAAR weakness) and Mexican / Brazilian / Vietnamese slab finds a tariff workaround, HRC could roll back to $800/ton or lower. CLF Adj. EBITDA at $800 HRC is ~$700M (vs. $1.5B base case), and the equity compresses to single-digits. Probability: **30%**. Magnitude: -30 to -50%.

### 10.2 Auto demand weakness

Auto is 28% of CLF revenue. A 2026 SAAR below 15M units, or specific Big-3 plant idlings, hits CLF disproportionately. Tariff-driven OEM reshoring from Mexico is the offset — but if Mexico-US auto trade gets *resolved* rather than tariffed, the offset goes away. Probability: **20%**. Magnitude: -10 to -20%.

### 10.3 Weirton ramp delays

First-of-its-kind plant + tinplate-to-transformer conversion + new union workforce. The probability of slipping from 1H 2026 to 2H 2026 or even into 2027 is non-trivial. Probability of a ≥6-month slip: **35%**. Magnitude: -5 to -10% (mostly multiple compression on the structural leg).

### 10.4 New GOES competition

The biggest *structural* bear is Nippon-controlled US Steel deciding to restart electrical-steel capacity in the US (USS historically had Granite City and East Chicago electrical-steel capability). With Nippon's parent-company GOES expertise, USS could in theory re-enter US GOES production in 3-5 years. The NSA golden share creates friction here but doesn't preclude it. Also: new GOES capacity from JFE or POSCO inside the US tariff perimeter (greenfield). Probability of materially competitive new US GOES capacity by 2030: **25%**. Magnitude on CLF specialty leg multiple: -2 to -3 turns.

### 10.5 Tariff reversal

Section 232 has survived multiple administrations, but it's policy, not law. A 2027+ administration could weaken or remove derivative-products coverage on transformers, which would compress Weirton economics. Probability through 2030: **20%**. Magnitude: -10 to -15% to Weirton SOTP.

### 10.6 Tax-credit changes

DOE grant for Butler is committed but disbursement is conditional. 45X eligibility for transformers/electrical steel is contested. Loss of any tax credit changes expansion economics. Probability of material change: **15%**. Magnitude: -3 to -5%.

### 10.7 Balance sheet

Net debt at ~$7.3B against trough EBITDA of $37M is the scary number. CLF's leverage covenants and refinancing wall (next major maturity 2027) ^[ambiguous] are an existential risk *only* in a HRC-back-to-$700 scenario. In a normal-cycle scenario, net debt comes down by ~$1B/year. But in a bear: equity gets repriced as a partially-impaired call option.

### 11.1 Beta / volatility profile

- **Implied beta**: ~1.7-2.0 ^[ambiguous] — among the highest in the AI-infra basket.
- **30-day realized vol**: typically 50-70% in normal markets, 80-100%+ at cycle inflections. ^[ambiguous]
- **Correlations**: ~0.7-0.8 with NUE and STLD ^[ambiguous]; ~0.6 with X (now constrained); ~0.4 with ETN/HUBB; ~0.3 with hyperscaler equities.

### 11.2 Sizing

For a basket built around the [[ai-data-center-buildout-investment-memo]] thesis, CLF should be a **smaller weight than POWL/ETN/HUBB**. Suggested:

- POWL / ETN: 5-8% each
- HUBB / Hitachi Energy ADR: 3-5%
- **CLF: 2-3%** (smaller, despite a wider P&L upside, because the variance is wider and the correlation with macro/auto is much higher)
- Pair: long CLF, light short on a basket of auto-OEM equities (F, GM, STLA) to hedge auto-cyclical exposure while keeping the GOES + tariff + reshoring legs.

### 11.3 When to add / when to trim

- **Add** on HRC index dips below $850 with auto OEM 2027 model-year ordering still intact.
- **Trim** on HRC spikes above $1,150 (cycle peak risk), or on Weirton ramp delays ≥6 months, or on USS-Nippon announcing US GOES capacity.
- **Avoid** in months immediately preceding scheduled debt refinancings (announce-effect drag).

Track per the [[risk-tripwires-and-warning-indicators]] framework:

- HRC index < $850 for 6+ weeks

- USS-Nippon announcing US GOES restart
